Project Overview

This project develops a Machine Learning–based early warning system for global food price crises. By combining historical food price data (WFP, 2016–2024) with country-level food insecurity scores (FAO), we train classification models to predict whether a country-product pair will experience a price surge of ≥ 20% within the next three months — a threshold aligned with the WFP Alert Price Spikes (ALPS) methodology.

The system is designed to give humanitarian organizations a 3-month head start before a crisis fully materializes, enabling proactive resource allocation and intervention planning.

Datasets

WFP Global Food Prices (2016–2024)

Monthly food price records across multiple countries and commodities. Initially ~2 million rows across 9 years.

Source: Humanitarian Data Exchange (HDX)

Variables: country, market, commodity type, price, unit, currency, date.

FAO Food Insecurity Dataset

Annual country-level prevalence of moderate or severe food insecurity — the macro-level vulnerability signal.

Source: World Bank Data360

Variables: country ISO3, year, food insecurity prevalence (%).

Combining micro-level price shocks with macro-level vulnerability allows the model to capture patterns that precede humanitarian emergencies. The final processed dataset contains 67,564 observations across 62 countries.

Preprocessing Steps

All preprocessing is performed by scripts/02_preprocess_data.py. The pipeline transforms ~2 million raw records into a clean, feature-rich panel dataset.

1
Load & Verify Data — 9 WFP annual files + 1 FAO file loaded with pandas. File paths and encodings verified.
2
Initial Inspection — Dataset shapes, column names, and missing values examined. Non-food entries and records with missing countryiso3, date, or price identified.
3
WFP Cleaning — Relevant columns selected and renamed. Dates parsed to datetime, prices cast to numeric. Non-food category removed.
4
Country Filtering — Countries with fewer than 4 distinct WFP years or fragmented coverage removed to ensure valid time-series calculations.
5
Monthly Aggregation — Records aggregated to country-product-month level: avg_price, median_price, max_price, price_std, obs_count.
6
Feature Engineering — Series aligned to monthly frequency with asfreq("MS"). Lag features (price_lag_1/3/6), rolling statistics, pct_change_1m, and volatility_ratio_3/6 computed. Extreme values clipped.
7
Target Variable — future_price_3m created by shifting 3 months ahead per group. crisis_label = 1 if future price increase ≥ 20%, else 0. Rows with missing future values dropped.
8
FAO Merge — FAO scores aggregated to country-year median; merged with WFP data on countryiso3 + year.
9
Final Filtering & Save — Countries with fewer than 4 FAO years or no FAO score removed. Rows with missing features dropped. Output saved to data/processed/processed_food_crisis_data.csv.

Class Imbalance

Temporal Train / Validation / Test Split

A temporal split (no random shuffling) was applied to prevent data leakage:

Train : 2016–2020 → 5 years (~55%) — model learns patterns
Val : 2021–2022 → 2 years (~22%) — model selection & threshold tuning
Test : 2023–2024 → 2 years (~22%) — touched only once, final report

The validation set is sized to match the test set (2 years each) so that validation AUC is a reliable proxy for test AUC. This was empirically verified — see the validation length comparison below.

Evaluation Metrics

Accuracy was not used — the dataset is class-imbalanced, and accuracy rewards predicting the majority class.

AUC-ROC — overall discrimination PR-AUC — minority class performance F1 Score — precision-recall balance Precision / Recall — false alarm vs missed crisis

The optimal classification threshold was selected on the validation set by maximising F1 across the precision-recall curve, then applied once to the test set. Interpretation

Price momentum features dominate across all models. Lag prices (price_lag_1, price_lag_3) and percentage price change (pct_change_1m) consistently rank as the most important predictors, indicating that crisis events are preceded by detectable price dynamics rather than occurring abruptly.

The FAO food insecurity score contributes meaningful signal, confirming that macro-level country vulnerability amplifies the impact of micro-level price shocks.

Seasonality (month) contributes moderate importance — it is relevant but secondary to price momentum signals. Product type is implicitly captured through the price dynamics of each commodity rather than through a direct product identifier.

The SHAP analysis confirms that the model's decisions are interpretable and consistent with domain knowledge, making it suitable for operational deployment in a humanitarian early warning context.

Discussion & Limitations

Overall Discussion

The results demonstrate that food price crises can be predicted 3 months in advance with meaningful accuracy. XGBoost (tuned) achieved the best trade-off between precision and recall on both the validation and test sets. Price momentum features (lag prices, percentage change, volatility ratios) consistently emerge as the most important predictors across all models, suggesting that crisis events are preceded by detectable price dynamics.

The temporal train/val/test split effectively prevented data leakage: the model never saw future data during training or model selection. The 2-year validation window was empirically shown to be a more reliable proxy for test performance than a 1-year window.

Limitations & Threats to Validity

  • Data sparsity: FAO scores are annual and missing for some country-year pairs, limiting the dataset to 62 countries.
  • Hyperinflation confounding: In countries with general hyperinflation, the 20% threshold may be triggered by macroeconomic conditions rather than food-specific supply shocks.
  • Temporal coverage: Global disruptions (COVID-19 in 2020–2021, Russia-Ukraine war in 2022) may create distribution shifts that reduce generalisability to future periods.
  • Product aggregation: Monthly aggregation across markets within a country masks local price heterogeneity that could be relevant for targeted interventions.
