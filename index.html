The Problem

A 20% price increase in a staple commodity within a 3-month window represents a statistically significant market shock — one our model is designed to detect before it manifests at the household level.

When food prices spike sharply, households can no longer afford sufficient nutrition. But humanitarian organizations often respond after the crisis has already materialized. We asked: what if we could see it coming?

We define a crisis event as a ≥ 20% increase in a country-product's average price within a 3-month future window — aligned with the WFP Alert Price Spikes (ALPS) methodology, which classifies such rapid escalations as "Market Shocks" that disrupt household purchasing power. By combining 9 years of monthly price data with country-level food insecurity scores, we train classification models to raise an alarm before the damage is done.

Research Question 1

Which countries and food products experience the highest price volatility, and are these patterns seasonal?

Using engineered features such as volatility ratios and the monthly variable to differentiate expected seasonal fluctuations from actual market anomalies.

Research Question 2

Using past price movements and a country's current food insecurity score, can a food price crisis in a country be predicted 3 months in advance?

Combining micro-level price momentum with macro-level FAO vulnerability to train classification models that raise an alarm before a crisis materializes.

Research Question 3

What are the most effective factors in determining crisis risk: country vulnerability, or seasonality?

Analyzing feature importance and SHAP values to identify the underlying drivers — making the model interpretable for policymakers.

Data Pipeline

1
Load & Verify — 9 WFP annual CSV files + FAO file loaded and validated by scripts/01_load_data.py.
2
Clean WFP — Non-food entries removed; missing prices and dates dropped. Columns renamed for consistency.
3
Filter Countries — Countries with fewer than 4 WFP data years or fragmented coverage excluded to ensure valid time-series calculations.
4
Monthly Aggregation — Records grouped to country-product-month level: avg_price, median_price, price_std, obs_count.
5
Feature Engineering — Lag prices (1, 3, 6 months), rolling volatility ratios, and monthly percentage change computed per group with asfreq("MS") alignment.
6
Target Variable — crisis_label = 1 if average price rises ≥ 20% in the next 3 months; else 0. Future-price column dropped after labelling to prevent leakage.
7
FAO Merge & Final Filter — Annual FAO scores merged by countryiso3 + year. Countries with fewer than 4 FAO years removed. Final dataset: 67,564 observations · 60 countries.
01

Which countries and food products experience the highest price volatility, and are these patterns seasonal?

Before building a forecasting model, it is crucial to understand fundamental market behaviors in different regions. Anomalous volatility may indicate systemic supply chain problems distinct from natural agricultural cycles.

Countries at Chronic Risk

The FAO food insecurity score reveals which countries face structural vulnerability — where any price shock lands on already stretched households.

Fig. 1 Countries ranked by chronic food vulnerability. Average FAO food insecurity score per country (2016–2024). Countries at the top spend the highest share of household income on food and have the least cushion against price shocks. Afghanistan, Ethiopia, and Sierra Leone consistently lead — these are the markets where our 3-month early warning delivers the greatest humanitarian value.

Which Products Trigger the Most Crises?

Bubble size = total crisis count. Axes show price volatility vs. country food insecurity. The most dangerous commodities combine both dimensions.

Fig. 2 Staple grains sit in the highest-risk zone. Each bubble = one food product; size = total crisis events; x-axis = price volatility; y-axis = country vulnerability. Staple grains (maize, wheat, rice) cluster in the upper-right — combining frequent large price swings with structurally food-insecure populations. These are the commodities a monitoring system must watch first.

Fig. 3 Prices accelerate before a crisis is declared. Monthly price changes during crisis (orange) vs. non-crisis (blue) periods. The crisis distribution is wider, more right-skewed, and shows heavier tails — meaning prices are already moving fast before a crisis officially begins. This accelerating pattern is exactly what our lag features are designed to detect.

Fig. 4 The predictive signal exists before any model is trained. Pearson correlations between each feature and the crisis label. Price lags and volatility ratios rank highest. This is model-free evidence that our feature engineering captured the right patterns — the information needed to predict a crisis is genuinely present in the data.

Distribution of Crisis Events

Fig. 5 Accuracy is a misleading metric here. Only ~15–20% of records are crisis events. A model that always predicts "no crisis" would score 80%+ accuracy while missing every actual crisis. This class imbalance is why we use AUC-ROC, PR-AUC, and F1 throughout — metrics that specifically penalize missing the rare but critical event.

Annual Crisis Intensity by Country

Fig. 6 Global shocks leave unmistakable signatures in price data. Share of a country's tracked commodities in crisis per year, for the 25 highest-risk countries. The 2020 horizontal band (COVID-19 supply-chain shock) and 2022–2023 band (Russia-Ukraine conflict, global grain price surge) are clearly visible across multiple countries simultaneously. Machine learning can learn to anticipate these real-world catastrophes — they are not statistical noise.

Seasonal Patterns

Fig. 7 Seasonality is real, but it is not the whole story. Left: monthly crisis rate vs. annual average (red = above average). Right: monthly price volatility by month. Lean seasons and harvest cycles create predictable windows of elevated risk. The effect is genuine but modest — calendar-based prediction alone would not be sufficient. We include month as a model feature to capture this residual signal.

Price momentum matters more than the calendar month — but ignoring seasonality leaves signal on the table.

02

Using past price movements and a country's current food insecurity score, can a food price crisis be predicted 3 months in advance?

Past price movements capture the economic momentum of a product, while the FAO food insecurity score provides macro-level vulnerability context. By combining these micro and macro indicators, we train classification models to provide an actionable head start for humanitarian interventions.

Features Used

avg_priceprice_lag_1 price_lag_3price_lag_6 pct_change_1mvolatility_ratio_3 volatility_ratio_6fao_score month

Target: crisis_label — 1 if price rises ≥ 20% in the next 3 months.

No Random Splits — Strict Temporal Split

Shuffling time-series data lets future information contaminate training — a form of data leakage that inflates apparent performance but fails on real future data. We apply a strict temporal split where the model never sees data from after its training window:

Train : 2016–2020 → 5 years (~55%) — model learns from history
Val : 2021–2022 → 2 years (~22%) — model selection & threshold tuning
Test : 2023–2024 → 2 years (~22%) — touched only once, final report

Why a 2-Year Validation Window?

Fig. 8 A 2-year window makes model selection more reliable. The val-to-test AUC gap measures how well validation performance predicts actual test performance. A smaller gap = our model selection decisions hold on future unseen data. The 2-year window is consistently more stable than 1-year, giving us confidence that threshold choices made on validation data will generalize.

Why Not Accuracy?

With ~80% non-crisis records, a model that always predicts "no crisis" scores 80% accuracy — and misses every real event. We use AUC-ROC, PR-AUC, and F1 instead — metrics specifically suited to imbalanced classification where the minority class is the one that matters.

Model Comparison — Validation Set

Fig. 9 Hyperparameter tuning delivers measurable gains. AUC-ROC and PR-AUC for all five models on the 2021–2022 validation set. XGBoost (tuned) leads on both metrics. The gap between tuned and default XGBoost shows that 40-iteration random search with TimeSeriesSplit translates directly into better crisis detection — the tuning was worth the effort [Chen & Guestrin, 2016].

Threshold Optimisation

In a humanitarian context, a missed crisis costs far more than a false alarm: missing a genuine crisis (false negative) carries a far higher human cost than raising a precautionary alert (false positive). This asymmetry justifies prioritising Recall over Precision and using F1 as the threshold-selection criterion. We select the threshold that maximises F1 on the validation set, then apply it once to the test set.

Final Test Set Results

Fig. 11 How many crises did the model catch? Confusion matrix on the 2023–2024 test set at the optimal threshold. True Positives (top-left) = crises flagged 3 months in advance. False Negatives (bottom-left) = missed crises — the most costly error in a humanitarian setting. The threshold was chosen to minimize missed crises while keeping false alarms operationally manageable.

Fig. 12 Definitive performance on completely unseen data. Final ROC curve of XGBoost (tuned), evaluated exactly once on the held-out 2023–2024 test set. The model had never seen this data during training or threshold selection. An AUC well above 0.5 confirms the model generalizes to future crises — not just the ones it was trained on.

How This Fits the Literature

Machine learning for food crisis prediction is a growing field. The closest precedent to this project is Andrée et al. (2020), who combined WFP food prices with food insecurity indicators and applied gradient boosted trees to predict crises 3 months ahead — reporting AUC-ROC of 0.71–0.82. Our model (AUC 0.767) replicates and validates this benchmark on a broader 60-country scope. Brych et al. (2025) developed an ML-based early warning system with the same framing as this project — integrating CatBoost, LSTM, and SHAP to forecast a composite Food Vulnerability Index across Ukraine, Moldova, and Serbia; their use of SHAP for interpretability and temporal validation structure directly parallels our methodology. Busker et al. (2024) forecasted food insecurity in the Horn of Africa using satellite and climate signals. Martini et al. (2022) used news streams as real-time early indicators. Kino et al. (2022) showed ML can guide interventions when survey data are unavailable. Driver et al. (2024) combined live price feeds with climate data for famine forecasting. Our contribution: a globally-scalable, price-signals-first framework validated across 60 countries.

Yes — food price crises can be predicted 3 months in advance. XGBoost (tuned) achieves the strongest precision-recall balance across both validation and test periods.

03

What are the most effective factors in determining crisis risk: product type, country vulnerability, or seasonality?

A high-performance model is only partially useful if it acts like a black box. By analyzing feature importance and SHAP values, we identify the underlying drivers of food crises to help organizations allocate monitoring resources more effectively.

A predictive model is only useful if we understand why it fires. We extract feature importance from all five models and use SHAP values — based on the unified game-theoretic framework by Lundberg & Lee (2017) — to get direction-aware, per-observation explanations for every prediction.

Feature Importance — All Five Models

Fig. 13 Logistic Regression (|coefficient| magnitude). Linear, interpretable baseline. Even in this simple model, price lag features dominate — the crisis signal is strong enough to be captured without complex interactions.

Fig. 14 Random Forest (mean impurity decrease). Non-linear ensemble, robust to outliers. Price lag 1 still leads, with volatility ratios prominent — consistent with the simpler model.

Fig. 15 XGBoost (default) — strong on structured tabular data [Chen & Guestrin, 2016]. Baseline before tuning.

Fig. 16 LightGBM — leaf-wise gradient boosting. Feature ranking is consistent with other tree models, reinforcing which signals are genuinely informative.

Fig. 17 XGBoost (tuned) — our final model. Feature weights shift slightly vs. default, but the overall story is preserved.

Cross-Model Comparison

Fig. 18 The same features lead across every model architecture. Side-by-side feature importance for all five models. price_lag_1 consistently ranks first or second. The FAO food insecurity score appears in the top features of every model. Features that rank high regardless of algorithm are the most robust crisis indicators — not artifacts of any single model's assumptions.

SHAP — Direction and Magnitude

Unlike aggregated importance scores, SHAP reveals how each feature pushes each individual prediction toward crisis or away from it.

Fig. 19 The model's reasoning, made visible observation by observation. SHAP beeswarm plot for XGBoost (tuned) on the test set [Lundberg & Lee, 2017]. Each dot = one observation; color = feature value (red = high, blue = low); x-axis = direction and magnitude of impact on crisis probability. High recent prices (red dots on the right) strongly push the model toward predicting crisis. The logic is transparent and consistent with domain knowledge — this is not a black box.

Fig. 20 Average contribution of each feature across all predictions. Mean absolute SHAP values. Unlike permutation importance, SHAP values are grounded in game theory and don't cancel positive/negative effects [Lundberg & Lee, 2017]. Price momentum leads; FAO score and month contribute consistently at lower magnitude.

Fig. 21 Country vulnerability amplifies the price signal. SHAP dependence plot for price_lag_1, colored by FAO food insecurity score. As recent prices rise, crisis probability increases — but high-FAO countries (red dots) cluster above the others. The same price shock generates higher crisis risk in an already food-insecure country. Vulnerability is not just background — it multiplies danger.

Price momentum dominates. Country vulnerability (FAO score) amplifies it. Seasonality contributes — but it is secondary to what has already happened in the market.

Conclusion

What we found — and what remains uncertain.

Food price crises leave detectable traces in price data weeks before they materialize at household level. By combining WFP price dynamics with FAO vulnerability scores and applying temporal machine learning, we can raise an alarm 3 months in advance with meaningful accuracy.

XGBoost (tuned) delivered the strongest performance on both validation and test sets. SHAP analysis confirmed that the model's logic is consistent with domain knowledge — lag prices and volatility are the primary drivers, with country vulnerability as an amplifier — making it potentially deployable in a real humanitarian monitoring context.

Limitations

FAO scores are annual and missing for some country-years, limiting the dataset to 60 countries and reducing global coverage.
In countries with general hyperinflation, the 20% threshold may be triggered by macroeconomic conditions rather than food-specific supply shocks.
Global disruptions such as COVID-19 (2020–2021) and the Russia-Ukraine conflict (2022) create distribution shifts that may reduce generalisability to future crises.
Monthly aggregation across markets within a country masks local price heterogeneity that could matter for targeted interventions.
